CHRONIC

HEPATITIS

B
General

Notes
Hepatitis

B

infection

is

caused

by

the

hepatitis

B

virus

(HBV)

and

can

either

be
acute

or

chronic.

Acute

hepatitis

B

is

a

new

onset

infection

that

may

or

may

not

be
icteric

or

symptomatic.

Acute

infection

is

self-limiting

with

recovery

usually
within

three

months

accompanied

by

clearance

of

hepatitis

B

surface

antigen
(HBsAg)

and

seroconversion

of

antibodies

to

hepatitis

B

surface

antigen

(anti-
HBs).

Chronic

HBV

infection

(CHB)

is

defined

by

persistence

of

HBsAg

for

six
months

or

more

after

acute

infection

with

HBV

and

encompasses

a

spectrum

of
diseases

that

includes

progressive

liver

fibrosis,

cirrhosis,

end

stage

liver

disease
and

hepatocellular

carcinoma.

Chronicity

is

common

following

acquisition

in
childhood.

Most

individuals

in

Zimbabwe

are

infected

in

childhood,

and

thus

most
adults

who

have

a

positive

HBsAg

have

chronic

infection.
Transmission
The

greatest

risk

of

developing

chronic

infection

is

following

MTCT

transmission
or

horizontally

in

childhood

(
see

section

on

EMTCT

of

HBV

for

additional
information
).

Generally,

HBV

is

predominately

transmitted

by

percutaneous

or
mucosal

exposure

to

infected

blood

or

body

fluids.

Accidental

inoculation

of
minute

amounts

of

blood

or

fluid

may

occur

during

medical

and

surgical
procedures,

use

of

inadequately

sterilized

syringes,

intravenous

and

percutaneous
drug

abuse,

tattooing,

body

piercing

or

sexual

transmission.

Transmission

in

the
healthcare

setting

is

now

mainly

historical,

although

continued

vigilance

is
paramount.
Diagnosis
1.

Rapid

diagnostic

test

to

detect

HBsAg

or

laboratory

based

serological
immunoassay.
2.

Transaminases

(ALT

and

AST)

may

help

determine

liver

inflammation.
3.

Additional

serology

markers

(HBeAg,

HBeAb

and

HBcIgM)

may

help
ascertain

the

stage

of

infection.
4.

The

degree

of

fibrosis

can

be

determined

non-invasively

using

the

aspartate
aminotransferase-to-platelet

ratio

index

(APRI).
