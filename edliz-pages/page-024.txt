Management

of

Asthma

(Adults

and

Children)
The

management

of

asthma

has

been

revised

to

include

the

use

of

new
formulations.

Please

note
:

it

is

recommended

to

use

inhaled

corticosteroids

in

all
asthmatics.

This

has

been

necessitated

by

the

observation

that

beta
-
2

agonists

on
their

own,

do

not

provide

adequate

therapy

for

all

asthmatics.

Asthma,

besides

the
bronchospasm,

has

an

inflammatory

component

which

needs

to

be

treated

with

the
inhaled

steroids.
Thus,

it

is

recommended

to

use

the

long-acting

beta-2

agonists

(LABA)

together
with

inhaled

corticosteroids

(ICS)

for

the

relief

of

an

asthmatic

attack.

Note:

Use
of

an

inhaled

short

acting

beta-2

agonist

(SABA)

e.g.

inhaled

salbutamol

on

its
own

is

no

longer

recommended.
Key

Changes

in

Asthma

Management
No

Asthmatic

Should

Be

on

Salbutamol

Monotherapy

as:


Salbutamol

(short-acting

beta-agonist

or

SABA)

only

addresses

bronchospasm
without

treating

underlying

airway

inflammation.


Over-reliance

on

SABAs

has

been

linked

to

increased

exacerbation

risk,

poor
asthma

control,

and

higher

mortality

rates.
What

to

Do

Instead?
Every

patient

should

have

a

controller

containing

inhaled

corticosteroids

(ICS)
or

an

ICS/LABA

(long-acting

beta-agonist)

combination

to

manage

the
inflammation

that

drives

asthma.


Symptoms

like

wheezing

or

breathlessness

signal

inflammation

in

the

airways,
which

salbutamol

alone

does

not

treat.
Guideline

Recommendation
:


Patients

must

always

take

an

additional

puff

of

ICS

every

time

they

use
salbutamol

for

symptom

relief.


This

ensures

inflammation

is

addressed,

reducing

the

risk

of

exacerbations

and
progression

to

severe

disease.
